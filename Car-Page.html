1954-1955

Early Mercedes F1 Era

Pioneering Excellence

Mercedes-Benz returns to Formula One with the technologically advanced W196, achieving immediate success under Alfred Neubauer's leadership with Juan Manuel Fangio and Stirling Moss.

In 1954, Mercedes-Benz returned to what was now known as Formula One (a World Championship having been established in 1950) under the leadership of Alfred Neubauer, using the technologically advanced Mercedes-Benz W196. The car was run in both the conventional open-wheeled configuration and a streamlined form, which featured covered wheels and wider bodywork.

Juan Manuel Fangio, the 1951 champion, transferred mid-season from Maserati to Mercedes-Benz for their debut at the French Grand Prix on 4 July 1954. The team had immediate success and recorded a 1–2 victory with Fangio and Karl Kling, as well as the fastest lap (Hans Herrmann). Fangio went on to win three more races in 1954, winning the championship.

The success continued into the 1955 season, with Mercedes-Benz developing the W196 throughout the year. Mercedes-Benz again dominated the season, with Fangio taking four races, and his new teammate Stirling Moss winning the British Grand Prix. Fangio and Moss finished first and second in that year's championship.

The 1955 disaster at the 24 Hours of Le Mans on 11 June, which killed Mercedes-Benz sportscar driver Pierre Levegh and more than 80 spectators led to the cancellations of the French, German, Spanish, and Swiss Grands Prix. At the end of the season, the team withdrew from motor sport, including Formula One.

During this first period of the team's participation in Formula One, Mercedes won 9 races in total, including three Grands Prix (the 1954 French Grand Prix, the 1954 Italian Grand Prix and the 1955 Italian Grand Prix) won by the streamlined "Type Monza", making them the only three races won by a closed-wheel car in Formula One history.

"The W196 was a masterpiece of engineering. Its dual configuration - both open-wheeled and streamlined - showed Mercedes' commitment to innovation and performance." — Alfred Neubauer

Before the start of the 2010 season, Mercedes-Benz's parent company Daimler AG bought a minority stake (45.1%) in the Brawn GP team with Aabar Investments purchasing 30%. Following the purchase of the team, as well as a sponsorship deal with Petronas, the team was rebranded as Mercedes GP Petronas Formula One Team.

The team has a complex history and its entry can be traced back to Tyrrell Racing, who competed as a constructor from 1970 until 1998, until being bought by British American Tobacco at the end of 1997 so its entry could be transferred to the then new constructor British American Racing (BAR) in 1999.

Team Mercedes GP hired German drivers Nico Rosberg and seven-time world champion Michael Schumacher, who returned to Formula One after a three-year absence. The team's performance during 2010 was not so competitive as under Brawn, with the team behind the leading three teams of Ferrari, McLaren, and Red Bull.

"We knew this was the beginning of something special. Mercedes had the vision, the resources, and the determination to build a championship-winning team." — Ross Brawn

2014-2016

Hybrid Dominance

V6 Turbo Era

The introduction of the V6 turbo hybrid power units marked a revolutionary change in Formula 1. Mercedes' engineering excellence shone through, with their power unit being significantly more powerful and efficient than their competitors.

In 2014, Mercedes dominated the first year of the V6 turbo era with 18 pole positions, 16 wins and 11 1–2s out of 19 races. The average winning margin to the nearest non-Mercedes competitor was 23.2 seconds.

The team continued their domination in 2015, improving on their impressive numbers from 2014 with 18 pole positions, 16 wins and 12 1–2s in 19 races. The average winning margin dropped to 19.7 seconds.

2016 saw Mercedes win the Constructors' Championship for the third consecutive season, winning 19 of the 21 races held, while securing 20 poles (the highest percentage ever in a single season of F1 at 95.2%) and 8 1–2s.

"The V6 hybrid era was our moment. We had the best power unit, the best chassis, and the best team. It was a perfect storm of engineering excellence." — Toto Wolff

From 2017 to 2021, Mercedes achieved an unprecedented eight consecutive Constructors' Championships, a record that may never be broken. The team's ability to adapt to new regulations while maintaining their competitive advantage was remarkable.

In 2017, Mercedes won the Constructors' Championship for the fourth consecutive time, finishing the season with 12 wins out of 20 races, 15 poles, 4 1–2s and an average winning margin to the nearest non-Mercedes driver of 13.1 seconds.

The team debuted their "Dual-Axis-Steering" system during pre-season testing for the 2020 season. This allows the driver to change the toe of the front wheels by pushing or pulling on the steering wheel, optimizing the car for better tyre warming on the straights or better mechanical grip in the corners.

2021 marked the end of Mercedes' drivers' championship streak, with Hamilton being overtaken by title rival Max Verstappen on the last lap of the season-ending Abu Dhabi Grand Prix after a controversial safety car restart. However, the team still secured the constructors' title for a record-extending eighth consecutive season.

"Eight consecutive constructors' titles is a testament to our team's relentless pursuit of excellence and our ability to adapt to any challenge." — James Allison

2022 saw major rule changes, which reintroduced ground effect. Mercedes revealed a radical design with what was dubbed 'zero-pods'. The radical design did not pay dividends, as the team struggled to understand and unlock its full potential.

Additionally, the car suffered from aggressive porpoising, which was significantly reduced and "solved" ahead of the Canadian Grand Prix. Despite their performance struggles, Mercedes's reliability was "amazing", meaning their only three retirements of the season came from crashes or mechanical failures.

After continuing to struggle with their radical "zero-pods" design in the 2023 season, Mercedes opted to replace Technical Director Mike Elliott with James Allison, with Elliott becoming Chief Technical Officer. This change occurred on 21 April 2023.

The team admitted the W15 will have a completely different concept for the 2024 season with their intentions to be trying to catch up to pace setters Red Bull for their hope of claiming the championship or fighting for wins.

"The ground effect regulations were our biggest challenge yet. But every challenge is an opportunity to learn and grow stronger." — Toto Wolff

On 1 February 2024, Mercedes confirmed Hamilton would leave the team after 12 years, with the seven-time champion signing a multi-year contract to drive for Ferrari from 2025 onwards. Mercedes junior driver Kimi Antonelli was announced as his replacement on 31 August 2024.

The team enters a new chapter with George Russell taking on the role of team leader alongside rookie Andrea Kimi Antonelli. This represents a significant shift in the team's dynamics and approach to racing.

Mercedes continues to focus on innovation and technical excellence, with the W16 representing their latest evolution in Formula 1 car design. The team's commitment to pushing the boundaries of what's possible in motorsport remains unwavering.

"The future of Mercedes F1 is bright. We have the talent, the technology, and the determination to continue our legacy of excellence." — George Russell
